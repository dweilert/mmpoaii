Homeowner Communication Guide

Know your rights. Understand your communication templates. Learn exactly what to do — and when — in every situation.

Your Critical Deadlines

Missing a deadline can cost you the right to a hearing, defense, or legal protection. These are the most important dates in the homeowner timeline.

Keep these deadlines visible. Missing any of them can result in losing your legal protections. When in doubt, send your response immediately by certified mail.

Best Practice: Set Reminders

When you receive any notice from the Association, immediately write down the 30-day deadline on your calendar. Set a reminder for day 25 so you don't forget. Send responses by certified mail with return receipt — never rely on email alone.

When You Get a Notice of Violation

A Notice of Violation (NOV) means the Association believes you've broken a rule. Here's exactly what to do at each step.

The Association must follow a strict process before imposing any fine. Knowing your rights at each step can mean the difference between curing the violation cheaply and fighting an expensive fine.

Step Action What To Do Timeline
1 Read the NOV Check the date, specific rule cited, cure deadline, and your 30-day hearing deadline. Keep this letter — you'll need it. Immediately
2 Decide: cure or contest? If you agree with the violation, fix it by the cure deadline. If you disagree, send H4 Hearing Request. You can do both. First few days
3 Send H4 (if contesting) Mail by certified mail to the Board within 30 DAYS of NOV date. Even if you plan to cure, requesting a hearing gives you a formal chance to explain context and negotiate. WITHIN 30 DAYS
4 Board schedules hearing Board sends Hearing Notice (T9) with 10+ days advance notice, panel names, your right to bring counsel, and the evidence against you. After H4 received
5 Attend hearing Present your evidence, photos, witnesses, and arguments. You may bring a lawyer or friend. Listen to the Association's case and ask questions. 10+ days after T9
6 Receive decision Panel issues written decision stating findings, whether violation upheld, fine amount, and cure deadline. Keep this — you'll need it if you appeal. 14 days after hearing
7 Mediation (optional) Before arbitration or court, you may request mediation — it's lower-cost, faster, and often settles disputes. Not required but strongly recommended. After decision
8 Cure or comply If fine upheld, remedy the violation by the deadline and provide Board with proof (photos, permits, receipts). This protects you from repeated violations. Per decision deadline

What to Bring to Your Hearing

Document Your Case

  • Photos: before-and-after, comparison shots of compliant properties, evidence your home is now cured
  • Documents: permits, contracts, repair receipts, prior ARC approvals, emails from the Board
  • Your statement: write out your key points in advance so you stay calm and focused
  • Witnesses: a neighbor or professional can testify — not required but helpful
  • Counsel: optional — you have the legal right to bring an attorney
Tip: Request a Hearing Even If You'll Cure

If you plan to fix the violation anyway, you might think "why request a hearing?" Because if the Board finds the violation upheld even after you've cured, you can use the hearing record to argue the fine should be reduced or waived. A hearing also buys time to fix the problem.

H4 — Hearing Request

How the ARC Process Works

Most exterior changes require Architectural Review Committee approval. Understand the process to avoid costly rejections and delays.

What Requires ARC Approval

Nearly any exterior change needs approval before you start. This includes:

  • Fences, walls, and gates
  • Paint colors and exterior finishes
  • Additions, decks, and patios
  • Driveways, landscaping, and irrigation changes
  • Pools, spas, and water features
  • Solar panels and generators
  • Flagpoles and antenna
  • Roof color or material changes
  • Window and door replacements
  • Exterior lighting and fixtures

What Does NOT Require Approval

  • Portable basketball goals (not permanent)
  • Clotheslines in non-visible areas
  • Interior renovations (not visible from street)
  • Temporary holiday decorations (within guidelines)
  • Routine maintenance and repairs (paint matching original color)
Warning: Start Without Approval = Automatic Violation

Starting work before ARC approval is a violation — even if your project would be approved. The Association can require you to tear it down and file a violation. Always submit H1 first and wait for approval before breaking ground.

The 45-Day Clock Rule

Once ARC confirms it has received your complete application, a 45-day clock starts ticking. Here's what happens:

Scenario What Happens Your Next Step
Application is complete ARC has 45 days to approve or deny. If ARC does not act within 45 days, your project is deemed approved. You can proceed after day 45 if no decision issued.
Application is incomplete ARC has 10 business days to send incompleteness notice listing every missing item. The 45-day clock does NOT start. Respond with H2 Resubmission, addressing every deficiency.
Application is denied ARC must state specific reasons per statute § 209.00505. Generic "does not conform" is not sufficient. Resubmit with H2 showing how you've addressed their concerns, or request informal conference.
Important: The Association cannot hide behind "incomplete" to avoid the 45-day deadline. Their incompleteness notice must be specific and detailed. If they issue a vague rejection, challenge it.

What If ARC Denies or Declares Incomplete?

If ARC sends an incompleteness notice, read it carefully. They must list every missing item. Use H2 to address each one:

  • Add the specific documents or details they requested
  • Include a cover letter explaining each addition
  • A new 45-day clock starts after they acknowledge complete receipt

If ARC denies your application, their denial must cite a specific rule or design standard. Generic rejections are not valid under the statute. If their denial is unclear:

  • Request an informal conference with the ARC chair to discuss the concerns
  • Modify your plan to address their stated objections
  • Resubmit with H2 explaining how you've complied
Statute Reference

§ 209.00505 requires ARC denials to state specific reasons related to the design standards in your CC&Rs. Arbitrary or vague denials may be legally invalid.

Your Disability Rights

You have strong legal protections when you have a disability. Understand the difference between accommodations and modifications.

The Fair Housing Act and Texas Property Code give homeowners with disabilities the right to request changes to rules or physical modifications. The Association must respond within 14 days.

Reasonable Accommodation (H5)

Change to a RULE or POLICY

  • Waiving a pet restriction for an assistance animal
  • Parking exception for wheelchair van
  • Waiving green-space percentage for medical equipment
  • Exception to noise restrictions for medical equipment

Use H5 when you need the Association to waive or change a rule for you.

Reasonable Modification (H6)

Physical change to YOUR DWELLING

  • Grab bars and railings
  • Wider doorways for wheelchair access
  • Wheelchair ramp or accessible patio
  • Accessible kitchen fixtures and counters
  • Accessible parking or driveway

Use H6 when you need to physically modify your home.

Key Protections for Both H5 and H6

  • No diagnosis required: You do NOT need to disclose your specific diagnosis or disability. A healthcare provider's statement of necessity is enough.
  • 14-day response: The Board must respond within 14 days. Silence = approval in some jurisdictions.
  • ARC approval cannot be required: The Association cannot require ARC approval as a precondition for a disability accommodation or modification.
  • Reasonable conditions only: The Association may impose reasonable conditions (e.g., "install grab bars in a way that does not damage the home structure"), but cannot block the modification.
  • Costs: You bear the cost of modifications. The Association cannot charge fees for reasonable accommodations.
Tip: Document Everything

Keep copies of your H5/H6 request, the Board's response, and any communications with the Association. If they deny or unreasonably delay, you have documentation for a fair housing complaint or lawsuit.

Common Mistake

Homeowners often submit H1 (ARC Application) for disability modifications instead of H5/H6. This is incorrect and can delay your rights. Use H5 for rule changes and H6 for physical modifications — these have different (stronger) protections.

H5 — Reasonable Accommodation H6 — Reasonable Modification

Assessments, Payment Plans & Liens

Understand your rights before a lien is filed. Proactive action can prevent collection proceedings.

The Three-Step Process Before a Lien

Under Texas law, the Association must follow this exact sequence before filing a lien:

  1. Adopt T26: The Board must formally adopt a resale certificate (T26) policy stating assessment obligations.
  2. Offer T3 Payment Plan: The Association must offer you a written T3 payment plan covering at least 3 months. You can also propose H9.
  3. Send T5 Pre-Lien Notice: The Association must send a notice stating the delinquent amount, what it includes (principal, interest, late fees), and your right to dispute.

Only after these steps can the Association file a lien. If they skip or botch any step, the lien may be invalid.

What You Should Do If Delinquent

Act immediately — don't wait:

  • As soon as you fall behind, submit H9 Payment Plan Request. This shows good faith and triggers the Association's obligation to work with you.
  • The Association cannot force collection or file a lien if you have a pending or active payment plan.
  • If you think the amount is wrong, submit H11 Assessment Dispute to challenge the balance.
Critical: Payment Applies to Assessments First

Under § 209.0063, whenever you make a payment on a delinquent account, it must be applied to the assessment FIRST, then to interest, then to late fees and collection costs. Never allow the Association to apply your payment to late fees first — that violates the statute.

Homestead Foreclosure Protection

Texas law protects your primary homestead from foreclosure for delinquent HOA assessments. Here are the limits:

  • Delinquency must exceed $2,500: The amount owed (excluding interest, fees, and costs) must be more than $2,500 for foreclosure to proceed.
  • Judicial foreclosure required: The Association cannot use a non-judicial (power of sale) foreclosure. They must file in district court, which gives you time to respond.
  • You have the right to cure: You can pay off the delinquency, plus costs, at any time before the foreclosure sale to stop it.
Important: This protection does NOT apply if your property is not your primary homestead or if you have a second home. Check your situation carefully.

After You Pay — Lien Release

Once you pay the delinquency in full, the Association has 30 days to record a release of lien. If they don't:

  • Send a certified mail notice demanding release
  • If they don't comply, you may have a claim for damages
  • A lien on your credit or title blocks sales and refinancing

Selling or Renting Your Home

Different rules apply depending on whether you're selling or renting. Know the timelines and requirements to avoid transaction delays.

Selling Your Home (H10)

  • Timeline: Request at least 15 business days before closing
  • Association response: They have 10 business days to respond
  • Buyer protection: Your buyer has a 5-day cancellation right if they don't receive the certificate before signing the contract
  • Fee: The Association may charge a fee (capped by statute). Budget $100–300.
  • Valid for 6 months: The certificate is good for 6 months from issue date
  • What's in the certificate: Current assessment amount, resale disclosures, lien status, governing documents

Renting Your Home (H18)

  • Minimum lease: 6 months minimum — no exceptions per the CC&Rs
  • Short-term rentals prohibited: Airbnb, VRBO, and similar platforms are prohibited. Listing on such a platform is itself a violation.
  • Timeline: Submit H18 within 14 DAYS of the lease start date
  • Tenant disclosures: You must provide your tenant with a copy of the Declaration, Bylaws, and Rules BEFORE they move in
  • Association approval: The Association cannot require approval of your specific tenant — they can only verify the lease meets the 6-month rule
  • You remain liable: You are responsible for your tenant's violations of the CC&Rs

Selling: Step by Step

  1. As soon as you have a purchase agreement, send H10 to the Association with the request date and target closing date.
  2. The Association will compile the resale certificate including current balance, disclosures, and governing documents.
  3. Your buyer receives the certificate and has a 5-day right to cancel if the disclosures are unsatisfactory.
  4. The closing happens after the buyer approves. Your outstanding balance (if any) is typically paid from sale proceeds.
Don't Miss the Deadline

Requesting H10 too late (less than 15 business days before closing) can delay your transaction or give your buyer grounds to back out. Always request early — you can request before you even have a buyer lined up to be safe.

Renting: What You Must Do

  1. Before your tenant moves in, provide them with a complete copy of the Declaration, Bylaws, and Rules. This is your legal obligation.
  2. Have your tenant sign an acknowledgment that they received these documents.
  3. Within 14 days of lease start, submit H18 with the tenant's name, lease term (must be 6+ months), and move-in date.
  4. Inform your tenant of any lease violations. You remain liable if they violate the CC&Rs.
Critical: No Short-Term Rentals

The CC&Rs explicitly prohibit short-term rentals. If you list your home on Airbnb or VRBO even once, that is a violation — even if no one books it. You can be fined. If you want to rent, it must be a residential lease of 6+ months.

Neighbor Disputes: When & How to Escalate

Not all neighbor problems require the Board. Learn when to address issues directly and when to involve the Association.

The CC&Rs require you to attempt to resolve neighbor-to-neighbor violations before filing a Board complaint. Understanding the escalation path can save time and preserve community relationships.

H17 — Neighbor Courtesy Request

Friendly, informal, no formal process

  • Best for: minor concerns, first approach
  • Tone: polite, collaborative
  • No deadline: your neighbor can respond whenever
  • No Board involvement: stays between neighbors
  • Examples: noise, parking courtesy, fallen branches, loose pet

Start here for neighborly issues — most are resolved at this stage.

H16 — Owner-to-Owner Violation Notice

Formal CC&Rs violation, required first step

  • Best for: ongoing violations, safety concerns, repeat issues
  • Tone: formal, references specific CC&Rs section
  • 10-day deadline: your neighbor has 10 days to cure
  • Precondition: Art. XII §1 requires H16 BEFORE Board complaint
  • Examples: unauthorized structure, chronic noise, property neglect

Use this if H17 doesn't work or the violation is serious.

Escalation Path

  1. H17 (optional): Send a friendly courtesy request. No formal requirements. Give them a chance to fix it.
  2. H16 (required): If H17 doesn't work, send a formal Owner-to-Owner Violation Notice citing the specific CC&Rs. Give 10 days to cure.
  3. Board complaint: After H16 and the 10-day period expires, you can file a complaint with the Board. Send documentation showing you attempted H16 first.
  4. T8 enforcement: The Board sends an official violation notice. The neighbor has 30 days to cure before a fine is imposed.
Statute Requirement

Article XII §1 of the CC&Rs requires that any owner-to-owner violation dispute be addressed between owners first (via H16) before the Board becomes involved. Skipping H16 may invalidate your Board complaint.

What to Include in H16

  • Specific CC&R section or rule being violated (get the exact section number)
  • Description of what you observed (dates, times, details)
  • Photos or evidence if possible
  • A reasonable cure deadline (typically 7–10 days)
  • A clear request for corrective action
  • Your contact information for their response
Send by certified mail with return receipt: This proves you sent H16 and when. This documentation protects you if the Board later investigates whether you followed the required process.
H16 — Owner-to-Owner Violation Notice H17 — Neighbor Courtesy Request

Property Exceptions & Fire Safety

How to request a formal variance from CC&Rs restrictions, and how to get Board approval for Firewise brush clearing on Common Area.

H7 — Deviation Request (Variance from CC&Rs)

A deviation is different from an ARC Application. An ARC Application covers improvements and modifications that are permitted by the CC&Rs — the ARC just reviews the design. A deviation is needed when you want an exception to the restriction itself — for example, requesting to build a fence taller than what the CC&Rs allow, or use a material that is normally prohibited.

Key Distinction: ARC vs. Deviation

ARC Application (H1): "I want to build a structure the CC&Rs allow — please approve my plans."
Deviation Request (H7): "I want to do something the CC&Rs restrict — please grant me an exception."
A deviation requires a 51% member vote. An ARC application is decided by the ARC committee alone.

How the Deviation Process Works

  1. Submit H7 at least 60 days before your desired start date — the Board must schedule a membership vote.
  2. Board places on agenda: The deviation request must be noticed to all members before a vote can be taken.
  3. 51% member approval required: A majority of all voting members (not just those present) must approve.
  4. Written approval issued: If approved, you receive written authorization. Keep a copy with your property records.
Important Deadline

Submit H7 at least 60 days before your desired start date. The voting and notice process takes time — do not begin work without written approval.

H3 — Firewise Brush Clearing Request

If you want to clear brush, vegetation, or debris on Common Area for fire safety purposes, you must get Board approval first. Common Area is Association property — clearing it without authorization is a violation, even if your intent is to reduce fire risk.

Do Not Clear Common Area Without Approval

Even well-intentioned brush clearing on Common Area without Board authorization is treated as a violation. Always submit H3 and wait for written approval before touching any vegetation on Common Area.

Firewise Process

  1. Submit H3 describing the area, type of vegetation, and your fire safety justification.
  2. Firewise Committee review: The committee inspects the area and makes a recommendation to the Board.
  3. Board decision within 30 days: The Board must act on the Firewise Committee's recommendation within 30 days.
  4. Written authorization: If approved, you receive written authorization specifying what may be cleared and how.
Tip: For your own lot (not Common Area), standard yard maintenance and fire mitigation does not require H3. H3 is specifically for requests involving Association-owned Common Area land.
H7 — Deviation Request H3 — Firewise Brush Clearing Request

Your Records Rights

Texas law gives you a broad right to inspect and copy Association records. Learn what you can request, how to ask, and what to do if the Board doesn't respond.

Under Texas Property Code § 209.005, homeowners have the right to inspect and copy most Association records. This includes financial records, meeting minutes, contracts, and more. H8 includes a comprehensive checklist of every category of record you can request.

What Records Can You Request?

✅ Generally Available

  • Annual budgets and financial statements
  • Board meeting minutes (after approval)
  • Annual meeting minutes
  • Contracts and vendor agreements
  • Reserve fund studies
  • Collection policy
  • CC&Rs, Bylaws, and Rules
  • Assessment rates and fee schedules
  • Insurance policies

⚠️ Restricted or Exempt

  • Individual homeowner account details (except your own)
  • Personnel files and employment records
  • Attorney-client privileged communications
  • Pending litigation records
  • Executive session minutes
  • Records of unresolved violations involving other owners

How to Submit H8

  1. Be specific: List each category or document you want — vague requests are easier to delay or deny.
  2. Choose inspection or copies: You may inspect in person (free) or request copies (the Association may charge a reasonable copying fee).
  3. The Association has 10 business days to respond to a records request under § 209.005.
  4. If denied: The Association must state in writing why the record is exempt. You may challenge an improper denial.
Statute — § 209.005

A property owners' association must make its books and records available for examination by a member or the member's authorized agent. The association has 10 business days to respond. Failure to comply may entitle you to seek a court order and attorney's fees.

If the Board Doesn't Respond

Document every attempt: keep a copy of your H8 letter and any delivery confirmation. If the Association fails to respond within 10 business days, you may pursue enforcement under § 209.007 and potentially recover attorney's fees if you prevail in court.

Pro tip: Send H8 by certified mail with return receipt. This creates a dated paper trail showing when your request was received, which is critical if you later need to prove the 10-business-day clock started.
H8 — Records Inspection / Copy Request

Meetings & Member Governance

Your rights to participate in Association governance — petitioning for meetings, running for the Board, voting by proxy, and adding items to the agenda.

As an Association member, you have meaningful rights to participate in how the community is governed. These templates help you exercise those rights formally and on the record.

H12 — Special Meeting Petition

Call a membership meeting outside the normal schedule

  • Threshold: Signatures from owners of at least 25% of lots
  • Board must call meeting: Within 30 days of receiving a valid petition
  • Meeting must be held: Within 60 days of the petition
  • Use when: Urgent community matter, Board recall, vote on major issue

H13 — Director Nomination

Run for the Board of Directors

  • Deadline: Submit 14 days before the Annual Meeting to be on the ballot
  • Floor nominations: You may also be nominated from the floor at the meeting
  • Eligibility: Must be a member in good standing (no delinquent assessments)
  • Election: All Board elections are conducted by secret ballot

H14 — Member Proxy Form

Vote when you can't attend

  • Deadline: Submit before or at the start of the meeting
  • General proxy: Your designee decides all votes
  • Limited proxy: You specify your vote on each item
  • Counts toward quorum: Proxy votes count toward the attendance quorum

H15 — Agenda Item Request

Add an item to a Board or Annual Meeting agenda

  • Deadline: Submit at least 7 days before the meeting
  • Board meetings: You also have the right to speak during the open comment period without a formal agenda item
  • Annual Meeting: Agenda items must be submitted in advance to be officially acted upon

When to Use Each Template

Situation Template Key Deadline
I want to force a membership vote on an urgent matter H12 Gather 25% of lot owner signatures first
I want to run for the Board H13 14 days before Annual Meeting
I can't attend the Annual Meeting but want to vote H14 Before the meeting starts
I want a specific issue discussed at the next meeting H15 7 days before the meeting
Your Right to Speak

Even without a formal H15 agenda item, you have the right to address the Board during the open owner comment period at any regular Board meeting. You do not need to submit any form to speak — just attend and request time to comment before any vote is taken.

Good standing requirement: To run for the Board (H13) or vote by proxy (H14), you must be a member in good standing — meaning no delinquent assessments. If you have an outstanding balance, resolve it or submit H9 for a payment plan before the election.

Account Updates & General Inquiries

Keep your contact information current and know how to reach the Board for questions not covered by a specific template.

H19 — Owner Address / Contact Update

If you move, change your mailing address, email, or phone number, you are required to notify the Association within 30 days. This is not optional — it protects you.

Why This Matters: Legal Notice Risk

Under Texas law, Association notices (including violation notices, lien warnings, and meeting notices) are legally effective when sent to your last address on file — even if you've moved and never received them. If you miss a lien notice because your address was outdated, you cannot claim you didn't receive it. Update your address immediately when you move.

What to Update with H19

  • Primary mailing address (if different from property address)
  • Email address for electronic notices
  • Phone number for urgent contact
  • Preferred contact method
  • Emergency contact (optional but recommended)
Non-resident owners: If you rent out your property and live elsewhere, keeping your mailing address current is especially critical. All legal notices will go to your address on file, not to your tenant's address.

H20 — General Inquiry

For questions or comments to the Board not covered by any other specific template, use H20. This creates a written record of your inquiry and gives the Board a formal channel to respond.

When to Use H20

  • Questions about a specific rule or policy interpretation
  • Requests for clarification on a Board decision
  • General feedback or suggestions for the community
  • Questions about upcoming projects or maintenance
  • Any communication that doesn't fit another template
Open Forum Rights

Remember: you also have the right to raise general questions in person at any Board meeting during the open comment period. H20 is for written inquiries you want on the record or when attending a meeting isn't practical.
