Phase 1 — Orientation

Module 1: What You Just Agreed To

The moment you were seated on the board, you became an officer of a Texas non-profit corporation with real legal duties. This module covers fiduciary duties, personal liability, conflicts of interest, and how to protect yourself.
⏱ ~30 min📋 6 items❓ 5-question quiz

The Three Fiduciary Duties

Under Texas Business Organizations Code Ch. 22, every board member owes three fiduciary duties to the membership:

  • Duty of Care — Make decisions with the care a reasonably prudent person would exercise. Read materials before meetings, ask questions, get professional advice on complex matters, and never vote on things you don't understand.
  • Duty of Loyalty — Act in the best interest of the association, not your own. If you have a financial or personal relationship with a vendor under consideration, disclose it and recuse yourself from that vote.
  • Duty of Obedience — Follow the governing documents and applicable law. You cannot vote to do something the CC&Rs don't authorize, even if you think it's a good idea — and you certainly cannot vote to do something Chapter 209 prohibits.
Legal Reference
TX Business Organizations Code Ch. 22 — Non-Profit Corporations. These duties apply from your first day and continue through your last.

The Business Judgment Rule — Your Primary Protection

The business judgment rule protects board members from personal liability for decisions that turn out to be wrong — as long as you made the decision in good faith, with reasonable information, and without a conflict of interest. It does NOT protect you if you voted without reading materials, had an undisclosed conflict of interest, acted in knowing violation of Chapter 209, or benefited personally at the association's expense.

Practical Rule
Document your reasoning. When the board takes a significant action, record in the minutes that you reviewed relevant information and exercised judgment in good faith. That paper trail is your protection if the decision is later challenged.

Personal Liability Exposure

Board members can be personally sued. While incorporation under BOC Ch. 22 provides a corporate shield, that shield has gaps. Personal liability may arise from: personally participating in a tortious act, making intentional misrepresentations to homeowners, acting outside the scope of board authority, committing fraud or gross negligence, or approving illegal discrimination under the Fair Housing Act.

⚠ Critical Point
Board members who knowingly violate Chapter 209 notice requirements — for example, filing a lien without the required §209.006 notice — may face both a lawsuit voiding the lien AND a personal liability claim. Ignorance of the law is not a defense.

Conflicts of Interest — The Most Common Ethics Trap

A conflict exists when a board member has a financial, personal, or professional relationship that could benefit from a board decision. Common examples: owning a company bidding on the HOA contract; being related to the property manager; voting on enforcement against a personal enemy; approving your own architectural modification. The solution is always the same: disclose and recuse — announce the conflict, leave the room, and have the secretary note it in the minutes.

Module 2: The Governing Document Stack

Every layer of HOA governance can only operate within the constraints set by the layers above it. A rule that contradicts a higher layer is legally void — even if the board passed it unanimously.
⏱ ~35 min📋 5 items❓ 5-question quiz

The Legal Hierarchy — Six Layers

LayerSourceWhat It Governs
1. Federal LawU.S. CongressFair Housing Act, FDCPA, ADA, Bankruptcy Code — override everything
2. Texas ConstitutionTexas votersHomestead protection (Art. XVI §50), due process (Art. I §19)
3. Texas StatutesTexas LegislatureCh. 209 (HOA operations), Ch. 202 (restrictive covenants), Ch. 207 (disclosure)
4. CC&Rs / DeclarationDeveloper / homeowner voteAssessments, land use restrictions, enforcement authority, interest rates
5. BylawsBoard / homeowner voteMeeting procedures, board composition, elections, officer duties
6. Rules & RegulationsBoard voteDay-to-day rules — parking, pool hours, pets, architectural guidelines
Most Common Board Mistake
Many boards spend years enforcing rules that are legally void because they conflict with Chapter 209 or Chapter 202. Know your statutes — not just your CC&Rs.

Chapter 202 — Applies to ALL Subdivisions

Chapter 202 applies to all Texas subdivisions with recorded deed restrictions — including voluntary HOAs. Key provisions: §202.006 requires all governing documents to be filed in county deed records; §202.003 says covenants are construed liberally; §202.007–.024 list specific things HOAs CANNOT ban — composting, rain barrels, solar panels, wind-resistant roofing, flag displays, religious items, security cameras, standby generators, political signs (with Election Code §259.002), and more.

Chapter 209 — The Homeowner Protection Act

Chapter 209 is the primary statute governing mandatory single-family HOAs in Texas. It sets minimum standards for governance, notice, enforcement, collection, and foreclosure. When the CC&Rs say one thing and Chapter 209 says another, Chapter 209 wins — always. This is true even if the CC&Rs predate the statute.

Key Principle
The board does not have inherent authority. If the CC&Rs don't authorize an action, the board cannot take it. And if Chapter 209 prohibits it, the CC&Rs cannot authorize it.

Module 3: Roles & Division of Authority

A board without clear role definition either has one person doing everything or five people arguing about authority. This module defines each officer role, what decisions need a full board vote, and the board–management company boundary.
⏱ ~25 min📋 5 items❓ 5-question quiz

Officer Roles

RolePrimary ResponsibilitiesCannot Do Alone
PresidentPresides over meetings, signs board-approved contracts, primary spokesperson, directs management companyApprove contracts, spend reserves, change rules, initiate enforcement
SecretaryTakes and maintains minutes, manages official notice, certifies documentsApprove minutes without board vote
TreasurerOversees financial accounts, presents reports, monitors reserves, reviews budgetApprove expenditures above board threshold, co-mingle operating and reserve funds
At-Large MembersVote on all matters, serve on committeesAct unilaterally on behalf of the board
Critical Point
The president has NO unilateral authority to make policy decisions or approve major expenditures outside of board meetings. A president who acts alone beyond defined authority is personally exposed and may have committed a fiduciary breach.

What Requires a Full Board Vote

Always requires a formal board vote: approving any contract above the board-defined threshold; adopting or amending any rule, fine schedule, or policy; authorizing a lien filing or legal action; approving or denying an architectural modification; setting assessment amounts; hiring or terminating the management company.

Typically delegable to president or management company (with board authorization): sending routine violation notices per adopted policy; signing routine contracts below the spending threshold; responding to homeowner account inquiries; scheduling and noticing meetings.

The Board–Management Company Boundary

The management company works for the board — not the other way around. The board sets policy; the management company implements it. The board retains ultimate legal responsibility for everything that happens in the HOA's name. You cannot delegate your fiduciary duties to a management company. Establish in writing: spending authority threshold, enforcement autonomy limits, public communication authority, and legal referral trigger.

Legal Reference
TX Property Code §209.0052 governs management company contracts — term limits and required termination rights. Covered fully in Module 7.

Module 4: Running a Legal Meeting

A board meeting that violates Chapter 209's open meeting requirements can void the actions taken at that meeting. This module covers every legal requirement for calling, noticing, running, and documenting a board meeting in Texas.
⏱ ~40 min📋 7 items❓ 5-question quiz

§209.0051 — The Open Meeting Requirement

Section 209.0051 requires that most board meetings be open to all homeowners. The board cannot hold secret meetings or take binding votes in private conversations, emails, or text messages. Key requirements: owners must receive notice at least 72 hours in advance (check bylaws — may require more); notice must include date, time, location, and agenda; homeowners have the right to attend and observe.

Legal Reference
TX Property Code §209.0051. Note: This section does not apply to HOAs already subject to Government Code Ch. 551 (Open Meetings Act) — a rare exception for very large quasi-governmental associations.

Executive Session — Permitted Topics Only

The board may close the meeting only for: pending or threatened litigation; contract negotiations; personnel matters; specific homeowner enforcement actions (to protect their privacy). Process: Announce you are going into executive session and state the authorized reason. Adjourn before voting — all votes must be taken in open session. Executive session discussions are not recorded in the minutes — only the fact and topic are noted.

What Cannot Happen in Executive Session
You cannot vote. You cannot discuss budget matters, rule changes, or vendor selection. You cannot conduct the entire meeting in executive session. Violations can void the actions taken and expose board members to personal liability.

Meeting Minutes — Your Legal Record

Minutes are the official record of every board action. Minimum content: date, time, location; names of members present and absent; quorum established; each motion — who made it, who seconded, vote by name (yes/no/abstain); conflict of interest disclosures and recusals; notation that executive session occurred (topic only); time of adjournment. Minutes are NOT a transcript of discussion — but for significant judgment calls, add a brief note on reasoning to support the business judgment rule defense.

Module 5: Budget & Financial Oversight

Financial mismanagement is one of the most common reasons HOAs face crises — and one of the most preventable. This module covers reading HOA financial statements, the critical difference between operating and reserve funds, budget adoption, and reserve studies.
⏱ ~45 min📋 6 items❓ 5-question quiz

The Two Buckets — Keep Them Separate

Operating Fund: Day-to-day expenses — landscaping, utilities, insurance premiums, management fees, minor repairs. Funded by regular assessments. Runs on an annual budget cycle.

Reserve Fund: Long-term capital replacement — roofs, parking lots, pool equipment, major structural components. Must be kept in a separate bank account and never raided for operating expenses.

⚠ Most Common Financial Error
Co-mingling operating and reserve funds in the same account — even with separate bookkeeping — is a serious breach. A court may pierce the corporate veil and hold board members personally liable for misappropriation of reserve funds.

Reading the Three Key Financial Reports

  • Balance Sheet: What the HOA owns (assets) and owes (liabilities) at a point in time. Watch for: reserve fund balance, delinquent receivables, outstanding payables.
  • Income Statement (P&L): Revenue vs. expenses year-to-date. Watch for: budget variance — any line item more than 10% over budget warrants explanation.
  • Delinquency Report: All owners with past-due balances, by amount and age. A delinquency rate above 5% is a warning sign.

Reserve Studies — The Board's Crystal Ball

A reserve study inventories all major common area components, estimates their remaining useful life, and calculates the annual contribution needed. Without one, the board is guessing on reserves. Target: 70% funded. Below 30% is a crisis zone that often triggers special assessments. Commission a full study every 3 years; update annually.

Best Practice
A reserve study costs $1,500–$5,000. A surprise major component failure without reserves costs 10–50x that in special assessments and homeowner conflict.

Module 6: Collections & Enforcement

Skipping a single required step in the Chapter 209 enforcement process doesn't just slow things down — it can void the entire action and expose the board to attorney's fees. This module covers every required step in order.
⏱ ~50 min📋 8 items❓ 5-question quiz

The Enforcement Flowchart

StepAction RequiredStatuteIf Skipped
1Written delinquency/violation notice — certified mail§209.006All subsequent steps voidable
230-day cure period (curable violations)§209.006Fine cannot be imposed
3Owner may request hearing within 30 days§209.007Fine challengeable
4Hearing held if requested (10 days notice)§209.007Fine voidable
5Offer written payment plan (delinquent assessments)§209.0062Lien filing challenged
6File assessment lien in county deed records§209.0094Lien has no legal effect
7Homestead: obtain court order authorizing foreclosure§209.0092Foreclosure void — constitutional violation
8Conduct foreclosure sale per Ch. 51Ch. 51Sale may be voided
Most Common Error
Sending the notice by regular mail instead of certified mail. §209.006 requires certified mail — no other method satisfies the statute.

Payment Plan Obligation — §209.0062

Before filing a lien, the HOA must offer a written payment plan over at least 3 months. The plan cannot include attorney's fees (unless the owner later defaults). New late fees are suspended while the owner complies. The only exception: the owner defaulted on a prior plan within the last 2 years.

Payment Priority — §209.0063

Partial payments must be applied in this order — the board cannot override this: (1) delinquent assessments, oldest first; (2) current assessments; (3) late fees and interest; (4) collection costs and attorney's fees. Some older CC&Rs specify fees-first — that order is illegal under §209.0063.

Foreclosure Hard Rules

  • Cannot foreclose for fines alone — only unpaid assessments support a lien
  • Homestead requires a court order before foreclosure (§209.0092 / TX Constitution Art. XVI §50)
  • Cannot foreclose if assessments owed are below the statutory minimum threshold — verify with attorney

Module 7: Contracts & Vendors

A board that doesn't understand contract basics will overpay, get locked into bad agreements, and expose the association to liability. This module covers §209.0052 contract limits, what to look for in every contract, and procurement best practices.
⏱ ~35 min📋 6 items❓ 5-question quiz

§209.0052 — Contract Limits

Texas Property Code §209.0052 places restrictions on HOA contracts. Key rules: management company contracts are subject to term limits; the association must retain a right to terminate with written notice; all material contracts must be approved by board vote at an open meeting.

Legal Reference
TX Property Code §209.0052. Verify the current version with your attorney — contract term limits have been amended in recent legislative sessions.

Contract Review Essentials

Must HaveMust NOT Have
Clear scope of servicesAuto-renewal without 30-day opt-out notice
Fixed price or price change capsUnilateral price increase rights for vendor
Insurance and indemnification clauseWaiver of HOA's right to terminate for cause
Term and termination rightsExclusivity preventing competitive bidding at renewal
Performance standardsBinding arbitration clause without board awareness

Procurement Best Practices

Establish a written procurement policy with spending thresholds. Suggested: under $1,000 — management company may approve; $1,000–$10,000 — president + one board member; over $10,000 — full board vote + 2 bids; over $25,000 — full board vote + 3 bids + documented selection rationale in minutes.

Best Practice
Document why you selected a vendor — especially when not choosing the lowest bid. "Selected Vendor B at $12,500 over Vendor A at $11,200 because of 10-year track record and $2M insurance coverage." That reasoning protects the business judgment rule defense.

Module 8: Insurance Coverage

Insurance is the board's primary risk transfer tool. Without the right coverage, a single lawsuit, theft, or accident can deplete reserves and expose board members personally. This module covers the four essential policies every HOA should carry.
⏱ ~30 min📋 5 items❓ 5-question quiz

The Four Essential Policies

PolicyWhat It CoversWho It ProtectsMissing = Risk
D&OPersonal liability of board members for decisions in official capacityIndividual board membersBoard exposed personally — don't serve without it
General LiabilityBodily injury and property damage claims on common areasHOA as entityOne slip-and-fall at the pool could bankrupt the association

D&O — Your Personal Protection

D&O insurance covers legal defense costs and damages when a homeowner sues a board member personally for decisions made in their official capacity. Without it, a disgruntled homeowner pursuing a board member for an improper enforcement action could reach personal assets.

⚠ D&O Coverage Gaps
Most D&O policies do NOT cover: intentional criminal acts, fraud, FHA discrimination claims (some do — ask your agent), and claims arising from failure to maintain required insurance. Review exclusions annually.

Module 9: Architectural Review Authority

Architectural review is one of the highest-risk governance areas — not because it's complex, but because boards regularly get it wrong in ways that trigger statutory violations and federal civil rights claims.
⏱ ~35 min📋 6 items❓ 5-question quiz

§209.00505 — The Written Denial Requirement

When the ARC denies a request: (1) it must be in writing; (2) it must state the specific reason; (3) it must state what changes would lead to approval; (4) the homeowner has the right to appeal to the full board. If the ARC fails to act within the time specified in the governing documents, it may constitute approval — check your CC&Rs.

⚠ Most Common ARC Error
Telling a homeowner verbally "we don't think it fits the neighborhood." That is NOT a legally sufficient denial. It must be written, specific, and include a path to approval.

FHA & ADA — The Override Rule

When a homeowner requests a modification for disability reasons, the ARC must approve it even if the CC&Rs would normally prohibit the change. This includes: wheelchair ramps, grab bars, widened doorways, reserved accessible parking, service animal pens. The FHA does not ask whether the CC&Rs permit the modification — it asks whether the modification is reasonable and necessary for equal enjoyment. If yes, approve it.

Legal Standard
Fair Housing Act reasonable accommodation and modification requirements override any conflicting ARC authority or CC&R provision. There is no HOA opt-out.

Module 10: Elections & Voting Rights

Improperly conducted elections are one of the most common grounds for HOA litigation. Chapter 209 establishes a detailed framework for HOA elections that cannot be waived by the CC&Rs.
⏱ ~30 min📋 5 items❓ 5-question quiz

§209.0059 — Voting Rights Cannot Be Stripped

Any CC&R or bylaw provision that disqualifies a homeowner from voting is void. The HOA cannot strip a delinquent homeowner's right to vote in board elections or on membership-wide votes — regardless of what the CC&Rs say.

Legal Reference
TX Property Code §209.0059. Limited exception: small developments (10 or fewer lots) with pre-2015 declarations.

Election Notice, Ballot & Recount Requirements

  • §209.0056: Election notice — no earlier than 60 days, no later than 10 days before the election. Must identify candidates and describe each matter voted on.
  • §209.0058: Ballots retained at least 1 year. Homeowners have the right to request access during this period.
  • §209.0057: Recount may be requested. Must be conducted by someone unaffiliated with management company or board.
  • §209.00594: Owners have the right to observe ballot tabulation.

Board Member Eligibility — §209.00591

Most provisions restricting a homeowner's right to run for the board are void. Bylaws may require that some — but not all — board members reside within the subdivision. Verify the current statute with your attorney regarding delinquency as a disqualifier, as the law has been amended.

Module 11: Homeowner Relations & Communication

How the board communicates is just as legally significant as what it decides. Board letters, emails, and notices are permanent records that can be used in litigation. This module covers legally sufficient notices, difficult situations, and communication best practices.
⏱ ~30 min📋 6 items❓ 5-question quiz

The Legally Sufficient Violation Notice

Every enforcement notice must contain: the specific rule or restriction violated (cite section); description of the violation (address, what was observed, date); the amount of any proposed fine or reference to the fine schedule; owner's right to cure and the cure deadline (30 days minimum); owner's right to request a hearing and the deadline (30 days); contact information for questions or hearing requests.

Template Saves Time and Legal Risk
Create one standard violation notice template with blanks for the specific violation, date, and fine amount. Run it by your attorney once. Use it for every notice. Consistency is your best defense against selective enforcement claims.

Tone Rules for All Board Communications

  • Never express personal opinions about homeowners
  • Never make threats beyond what the governing documents authorize
  • Never speculate about legal outcomes — that's what attorneys are for
  • Respond to complaints in writing — verbal responses create disputes
  • Never discuss pending enforcement actions on social media — ever
⚠ The Golden Rule of Board Emails
Before sending any email about a homeowner or dispute, ask: "Would I be comfortable if this email was read aloud in a courtroom?" If no — rewrite it. Board emails are official records subject to discovery in litigation.

Handling Difficult Situations

Hostile homeowner at a meeting: Allow them to speak. Listen without debating. When they finish: "Thank you. The board will take that under advisement and respond in writing." Do not argue, do not make promises, do not vote while emotionally reactive.

Social media attacks: Do not respond on social media. Issue a brief factual correction through official channels only if necessary. Consult attorney before responding to defamatory posts.

Records request under §209.005: Respond within 10 business days acknowledging the request. Produce records within 15 business days. Do not produce exempt records (pending litigation files, attorney-client communications).

Module 12: Working With Professionals

The board's three most important professional relationships — attorney, CPA, and management company — are cost-control tools when used properly. This final module covers exactly when and how to engage each, and what can never be delegated to any of them.
⏱ ~25 min📋 5 items❓ 5-question quiz

The HOA Attorney — Call Before, Not After

The most common and expensive board mistake: calling the attorney after taking a questionable action, instead of before. Call before: filing any lien or foreclosure; signing any major contract; amending CC&Rs or Bylaws; responding to any homeowner lawsuit or formal demand; making any FHA accommodation decision; taking any enforcement action you're uncertain about; receiving any government notice.

Cost-Control Rule
A 30-minute attorney call before acting typically costs $150–$300. A lawsuit because you skipped that call typically costs $15,000–$150,000. Use the attorney proactively.

The CPA — Annual Financial Oversight

Annual services should include: a financial review or audit (audit = highest assurance; review = appropriate for most HOAs; compilation = minimum, very limited assurance); tax return preparation (Form 1120-H or 1120); internal control recommendations — the CPA should flag any financial control weaknesses such as the same person authorizing and signing checks.

The Management Company — Active Supervision Required

The management company implements board policy — it does not set it. The board must maintain active oversight, not passive delegation. Annual check-ins should address: service levels vs. contract; all required filings completed on time; collection process following Chapter 209 exactly; vendor invoices reviewed before payment above the threshold.

What You Cannot Delegate
You cannot delegate board decision-making, fiduciary judgment, or legal compliance to the management company. If the management company violates Chapter 209 in your HOA's name, the board is still legally responsible.

Ongoing Training Requirements

Board training is not a one-time event. Texas HOA law changes almost every legislative session, and a board that doesn't track changes falls out of compliance without realizing it.

Annual Refresher — All Board Members, Every Year

A minimum two-hour session covering: any Texas legislative changes from the prior session; one deep-dive topic from the 12-module rotation; and a review of the HOA's own records and compliance status. Conduct this as a formally noticed open meeting and document attendance in the minutes.

Why This Matters
Texas Legislature meets every odd year. Chapters 202, 207, and 209 have been amended in virtually every recent session. HOAs that don't track changes may be enforcing outdated rules or missing new homeowner rights.

New Legislation Briefing — After Every Odd-Year Session

After each Texas legislative session (May of every odd year), the board's attorney or management company should deliver a written summary and a 60-minute briefing on new laws affecting HOA operations. Legislative updates can affect collection procedures, foreclosure authority, fine limits, disclosure requirements, and more.

New Board Member Orientation — Upon Seating

Every newly seated board member must complete Modules 1–3 (Phase 1 Orientation) before casting their first vote. Completion should be documented in board records using this program's certificate. Deliver orientation within 14 days of seating.

Annual Conflict of Interest Disclosure

Every board member signs a fresh Conflict of Interest Disclosure form at the start of each term or calendar year. Mid-year changes are disclosed in writing at the next board meeting and noted in the minutes.

Annual Insurance Review

The full board reviews all four insurance policies at least 60 days before renewal. The insurance agent presents current coverage levels and recommended changes. The board votes to approve coverage and documents the decision in minutes.

Reserve Study Update (Annual) / Full Study (Every 3 Years)

Review reserve fund status against the reserve study at every budget meeting. Commission a full reserve study at least every 3 years or after any major unexpected capital project.
