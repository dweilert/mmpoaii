Overview & Applicability §209.001–.003

What Chapter 209 is and who it covers
▼

Chapter 209 — the Texas Residential Property Owners Protection Act — is the primary state law governing mandatory single-family HOAs in Texas. It was named after Wenonah Blevins, an 82-year-old widow whose home was sold at foreclosure auction without her knowledge in 2001. The resulting public outcry led to the law's passage.

Who it applies to

Chapter 209 applies to residential subdivisions where membership in the property owners' association is mandatory — meaning owners are required to join as a condition of owning property in the subdivision. It applies to single-family home subdivisions, not condominiums (which are governed by Chapters 81 and 82).

Key Principle
When Chapter 209 conflicts with an HOA's CC&Rs or Bylaws, Chapter 209 wins. The statute cannot be waived or contracted around — even if the governing documents predate the law.

When it does NOT apply

Chapter 209 does not apply to HOAs subject to Government Code Chapter 552 (the Open Meetings Act) — a narrow exception for very large quasi-governmental associations. It also does not apply to subdivisions with 10 or fewer lots where the declaration was recorded before January 1, 2015 (for certain provisions).

Association Records Access §209.005

What owners can access and when
▼

Every homeowner has a statutory right to inspect and copy the association's books and records. This right cannot be limited by the CC&Rs.

What records are accessible

  • Meeting minutes (board and membership meetings)
  • Financial statements and budgets
  • Contracts to which the association is a party
  • Governing documents (CC&Rs, Bylaws, Rules)
  • Tax returns
  • Voting records and ballots (for 1 year after election)
  • Account records for individual owners (their own account)

What is NOT required to be disclosed

  • Attorney-client communications
  • Records relating to pending litigation
  • Personnel records of employees
  • Other owners' personal financial information
Board Obligation — Response Timeline
The board must respond to a records request within 10 business days of receiving it. Records must be produced within 15 business days. The HOA may charge a reasonable fee for copying but cannot charge for inspection.
Enforcement
An owner who is denied records access may bring an action in district court. The court may award the owner reasonable attorney's fees if the HOA wrongfully withheld records.

Open Board Meetings §209.0051

Meeting notice, owner rights, executive session rules
▼

Most board meetings must be open to all owners. The board cannot take binding votes in private conversations, emails, or text message exchanges.

Notice requirements

  • Written notice at least 72 hours before the meeting (check bylaws — may require more)
  • Notice must state: date, time, location, and agenda
  • Owners have the right to attend and observe

Executive (closed) session — permitted topics only

  • Pending or threatened litigation
  • Contract negotiations with a third party
  • Personnel matters
  • Enforcement actions against a specific owner (to protect their privacy)
What Cannot Happen in Executive Session
The board cannot vote in executive session. All votes must occur in open session. Budget discussions, rule changes, and vendor selection cannot be conducted in executive session. Violations can void the actions taken.

Required Notice Before Enforcement §209.006

What the HOA must send before imposing any fine or lien
▼

Before the HOA can impose a fine, suspend privileges, or take collection action, it must send a written notice that meets specific requirements. Skipping this step voids all subsequent enforcement.

The notice must contain ALL of the following

  • The specific rule or restriction violated (cite the section)
  • Description of the violation (address, what was observed, date)
  • The amount of any proposed fine or reference to the fine schedule
  • The owner's right to cure the violation (30-day minimum for curable violations)
  • The owner's right to request a hearing and the 30-day deadline to do so
  • Contact information for the HOA
⚠ Notice Method — Certified Mail Required
The notice must be sent by certified mail to the owner's last known address. Regular mail, email, or hand delivery alone does not satisfy §209.006. If the HOA sends by regular mail and the owner disputes receipt, the entire enforcement action may be void.

Curable vs. non-curable violations

Curable violations (e.g., overgrown lawn, unauthorized structure): Owner has 30 days to fix the problem. If corrected within the cure period, no fine can be imposed for that occurrence.

Non-curable violations (e.g., a past event that can't be undone): The cure period does not apply, but the written notice and hearing rights still must be provided.

Owner's Right to a Hearing §209.007

How to request and what happens at a hearing
▼

Every owner who receives an enforcement notice has the right to appear before the board and contest the action before any fine is imposed.

1
Owner requests a hearing in writing
Must be requested within 30 days of receiving the §209.006 notice.
2
HOA must schedule and notice the hearing
The board must provide at least 10 days written notice of the hearing date, time, and location.
§209.007(b)
3
Hearing is held — owner presents their case
Owner may bring evidence, witnesses, or an attorney. The board is not required to have an attorney present but may.
4
Board issues a written decision
The board must provide the owner with a written decision after the hearing. If the fine is upheld, collection may proceed.
Owner Tip
Always request a hearing in writing and keep a copy with proof of delivery. If the HOA fails to schedule the hearing or skips the required notice, any fine imposed may be unenforceable.

Right to a Payment Plan §209.0062

Mandatory offer before any lien can be filed
▼

Before the HOA can file an assessment lien, it must first offer the owner a written payment plan. This is not discretionary — it is a statutory requirement.

Payment plan requirements

  • Must be offered in writing before filing a lien
  • Must allow at least 3 months to repay the delinquency
  • Cannot include attorney's fees in the plan amount (unless the owner later defaults on the plan)
  • New late fees are suspended while the owner is in compliance with the plan

When the HOA is NOT required to offer a plan

  • The owner defaulted on a prior payment plan within the last 2 years
Owner Right
If you receive a delinquency notice, you have the right to request a payment plan before any lien is filed. Make the request in writing. The HOA must respond with a written plan covering at least 3 months.
Board Obligation
The offer must be made before filing a lien — not after. A lien filed without first offering a payment plan may be challenged and voided.

Payment Application Order §209.0063

How partial payments must be applied — cannot be changed by CC&Rs
▼

When a delinquent owner makes a partial payment, the HOA must apply it in this exact order. The CC&Rs cannot override this statutory order, even if they specify fees-first.

1
Delinquent assessments — oldest first
Past-due assessment amounts, starting with the oldest unpaid month.
2
Current assessments
The current period's regular assessment amount.
3
Late fees and interest
Accrued late charges and interest calculated at the CC&R rate.
4
Collection costs and attorney's fees
Legal costs and collection fees — applied last.
Common Violation to Watch For
Some older CC&Rs specify that payments are applied to fees and attorney's costs first. That order is void under §209.0063. If the HOA is applying your payments fees-first, document it and consult an attorney — this may affect your account balance calculation.

Assessment Liens §209.009

When a lien can be filed and what it covers
▼

An assessment lien is a legal claim recorded in county deed records that attaches to the property and must be satisfied when the property is sold.

What a lien can secure

  • Unpaid assessments
  • Interest on unpaid assessments (at the rate in the CC&Rs)
  • Reasonable collection costs and attorney's fees

What a lien CANNOT secure

  • Fines alone — the HOA cannot file a lien for unpaid fines when no assessments are owed. Fines can be collected through other legal means but not via assessment lien.
Pre-Lien Requirements
Before filing a lien, the HOA must: (1) send the §209.006 notice, (2) allow the cure/hearing period to run, and (3) offer a written payment plan under §209.0062. A lien filed without completing these steps may be void.
Two-Notice Rule (HB 886, effective 2024)
As of 2024, HOAs must send TWO notices before filing a lien: the first by first-class mail or email, and the second by certified mail. Both must precede the lien filing.

Foreclosure Protections §209.0092

Constitutional homestead protection against HOA foreclosure
▼

Texas provides the strongest homestead protections in the country. An HOA cannot simply foreclose on a home without court involvement.

Homestead foreclosure requires a court order

Before an HOA can foreclose on a homestead (primary residence), it must obtain a court order authorizing the foreclosure. This is a constitutional requirement under Texas Constitution Article XVI §50, reinforced by §209.0092.

An HOA can NEVER foreclose for fines alone

Only unpaid assessments — not fines, not late fees standing alone — can support a foreclosure action. Even then, the court order requirement applies.

⚠ If You Receive a Foreclosure Notice
Contact a licensed Texas real estate attorney immediately. You have legal rights that must be asserted in court. Do not ignore foreclosure notices — timelines are strict and missing a deadline can waive important protections.

Statutory minimum thresholds

Texas law establishes minimum delinquency thresholds below which the HOA cannot pursue foreclosure. Consult a Texas HOA attorney for the current threshold amounts — these have been adjusted by recent legislation.

Voting Rights §209.0059

CC&Rs cannot strip an owner's right to vote
▼

Any CC&R or bylaw provision that would disqualify a homeowner from voting in a board election or on any matter affecting owner rights is void.

Plain English
Even if you are behind on assessments, even if you have pending violations, and even if the CC&Rs say delinquent owners cannot vote — you still have the right to vote. The CC&R provision is simply unenforceable.

Limited exception

Small developments (10 or fewer lots) where the declaration was recorded before January 1, 2015, may have different rules. Verify with a Texas HOA attorney if this applies to your community.

Elections & Ballots §§209.0055–.00594

Notice requirements, observer rights, ballot retention
▼

Election notice — §209.0056

Written notice must be sent no earlier than 60 days and no later than 10 days before any election. Notice must identify candidates and describe each matter to be voted on.

Ballot retention — §209.0058

All ballots must be retained for at least 1 year after the election. Any owner may request access to inspect ballots during this period.

Right to observe — §209.00594

Any owner may observe the tabulation of ballots. The HOA cannot conduct ballot counting in secret.

Recount — §209.0057

An owner may request a recount. The recount must be conducted by someone unaffiliated with the management company or the board.

Board member eligibility — §209.00591

Bylaws may require that some (but not all) board members reside within the subdivision. Most other eligibility restrictions are void. Verify current law with your attorney as this section has been amended.

Architectural Review Authority §209.00505

Written denial requirements and appeal rights
▼

When the architectural review committee (ARC) denies a modification request, it must follow specific statutory requirements or the denial may be void.

A written denial must include

  • The specific reason for the denial
  • What changes to the application would lead to approval

Owner's right to appeal

Every owner whose ARC request is denied has the right to appeal that decision to the full board of directors.

FHA / ADA Override
When a homeowner requests an architectural modification for disability-related reasons, the ARC must approve it even if the CC&Rs would otherwise prohibit the modification. The Fair Housing Act and ADA override ARC authority on disability accommodations. There is no HOA opt-out.
Deemed Approval
If the ARC fails to act within the time period specified in the CC&Rs, the modification may be deemed approved. Check your CC&Rs for the action deadline.

Association Contracts §209.0052

Term limits and required termination rights
▼

Section 209.0052 restricts the HOA's ability to enter into long-term contracts that lock the community into unfavorable arrangements.

Key requirements

  • Management company contracts are subject to term limits
  • The association must retain the right to terminate contracts with written notice
  • All material contracts must be approved by board vote at an open meeting
Verify Current Limits
The specific term limits in §209.0052 have been amended in recent legislative sessions. Verify the current version of this section at statutes.capitol.texas.gov before signing any long-term contract.

Justice Court Jurisdiction §209.017

Owners can sue the HOA in justice court for Chapter 209 violations
▼

Added in 2021, §209.017 gives owners a lower-cost legal option for Chapter 209 violations.

What This Means for Homeowners
An owner may bring a lawsuit against the HOA for violations of Chapter 209 in the justice court of the precinct where the subdivision is located. Justice courts handle cases up to $20,000 and do not require an attorney. This makes it significantly easier and less expensive for homeowners to enforce their statutory rights.

Examples of violations that could be brought in justice court: improper lien filing (skipping §209.006 notice), wrongful denial of records access (§209.005), failure to offer a payment plan before filing a lien (§209.0062), incorrect payment application order (§209.0063).
